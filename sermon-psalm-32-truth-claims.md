## Truth-claims evaluation

Going through the thread point by point against the Hebrew text, the canon,
and 1689 LBCF theology.

### What checks out

**Narrative of 2 Samuel 11–12.** Accurate. King on the roof while the army
is at war; the sight, the sending, the taking, the staged death of Uriah,
the silence after.

**"Roughly a year" between the sin and the confession.** Defensible. The
text does not timestamp it, but pregnancy → birth → Nathan's visit fits the
traditional reckoning. About a year of silence is what the implicit timeline
gives.

**Nathan's parable as juridical/prophetic teaching.** 2 Sam 12:1–4. The
comparison to Jesus' parabolic method is interpretive but sound — Nathan
stands in the prophetic tradition Jesus inherits.

**"You are the man" (2 Sam 12:7).** Hebrew *attah ha-ish*. Exact.

**"I have sinned against the LORD" (2 Sam 12:13).** Hebrew *chatati
la-YHWH*. Exact.

**Maskil = teaching psalm.** Psalm 32's superscription reads *le-David
maskil*. The root *sakal* carries the sense of *instruct, give insight*.
"Teaching psalm" is well-attested in HALOT and NIDOTTE and is the dominant
reading. Other proposals exist (skillful song, contemplation) but the
didactic reading is mainstream.

**Three Hebrew words for sin in Ps 32:1–2.**

- **Pesha** (פֶּשַׁע) — transgression, willful breach. "Crossing a boundary
  you knew existed." Accurate.
- **Chata'ah** (חֲטָאָה) — sin, missing the mark. The verb *chata* is used
  in Judges 20:16 of slingers who could sling a stone at a hair and *not
  miss*. "Falling short of what you were made for." Accurate.
- **Avon** (עָוֹן) — iniquity, from *avah*, to bend or twist. "A twisted,
  bent condition. Moral distortion from the inside out." Accurate.

These three together form the classical Old Testament vocabulary of sin
and appear as a triad in Exodus 34:7 — the LORD's self-revelation
("forgiving iniquity and transgression and sin").

**Three Hebrew responses in Ps 32:1–2.**

- **Nasa** (נָשָׂא) — lifted, carried, borne away. Used of the scapegoat
  that *bears* iniquity (Lev 16:22) and of the Servant who "has borne our
  griefs and carried our sorrows" (Isa 53:4). Accurate.
- **Kasah** (כָּסָה) — covered. The plain verb means *covered*. The
  atonement weight ("covered with atoning provision") is theological, not
  lexical — it comes from the larger biblical theology of sacrifice and
  Paul's Romans 4 reading, not from the verb in isolation. Theologically
  right; the preacher must show the atonement link rather than asserting it
  from the dictionary.
- **Lo yachshov** (לֹא יַחְשֹׁב) — not reckoned. The verb *chashav* carries
  commercial and judicial weight; the LXX renders it with *logizomai*, the
  same word Paul uses for "credited" in Gen 15:6 and Rom 4. "A ledger
  term" is exactly right.

**Paul's use in Romans 4:7–8.** Direct citation of Psalm 32:1–2 from the
LXX. Load-bearing in Paul's argument that righteousness is reckoned to
faith apart from works. "Centerpiece" is rhetorical but defensible.

**"My bones wasted away" — balah (Ps 32:3).** *Balu atzamai*. *Balah* =
wear out, decay, like a garment (Deut 8:4 — "your clothes did not wear out
on you"). Accurate.

**"Your hand was heavy" — kabad (Ps 32:4).** *Kavedah alai yadekha*.
*Kabed* = heavy, weighty. Same root family as *kavod*, glory — that which
is weighty. Accurate.

**"David suffered more hiding his sin than confessing it."** Faithful
exposition of Ps 32:3–5.

### Two nuances to handle honestly

1. **Psalm 32's tie to the Bathsheba sin is traditional, not explicit.**
   Psalm 51 has the explicit superscription tying it to Nathan's visit.
   Psalm 32 does not. The Christian tradition reads Psalm 32 as David's
   later reflection — Psalm 51 the raw confession, Psalm 32 the teaching
   that comes out of having been forgiven. Defensible. But a careful
   preacher names the inference rather than asserting the connection as if
   the text itself made it.

2. **"Six Hebrew words"** is rhetorical thematic grouping, not literal
   count. There are far more than six Hebrew words in eleven verses. The
   grouping is *three for sin, three for grace*. Easy to handle — just
   frame it correctly.

### What is missing for a Reformed sermon

The thread stops at: *"God wants you to know He has a word for that.
Three, actually."* That is a fine landing for a social-media post. It is
not a gospel call. The Hebrew words are not the gospel; the Hebrew words
*describe* what God does at the cross of Christ. The sermon has to finish
what the thread leaves implicit and name Christ as the ground of each of
the three acts of grace.

- **Nasa** — Christ "has borne our griefs and carried our sorrows"
  (Isa 53:4, the verbs *nasa* and *sabal*); "he himself bore our sins in
  his body on the tree" (1 Pet 2:24).
- **Kasah** — Christ is the propitiation (Rom 3:25, *hilasterion* — the
  mercy seat that covers); his blood is the atoning covering the verb
  hints at.
- **Lo yachshov** — God does not reckon sin to the believer because he
  reckoned righteousness to him through faith (Rom 4:5, 22–25); Christ
  "was delivered up for our trespasses and raised for our justification."

### Verdict

**Solid.** Build the sermon.

## Sermon — "Psalm 32: lifted, covered, not counted"

**Text:** Psalm 32 (ESV)
**Confession:** 1689 LBCF
**Series slot:** Psalms — pairs with the existing *Psalm 51* manuscript

### Outline

1. The silence (vv. 3–4)
2. The breaking (v. 5)
3. The blessing — three Hebrew words for sin, three for grace (vv. 1–2)
4. Christ — where each of the three becomes real
5. Call

---

### 1. The silence

(Read Psalm 32:3–4.)

Most of you know the story. The army was at war. The king was on the roof.
He saw a woman, sent for her, took her, and when she conceived, he had her
husband killed to cover the trail. Then he went on being king. He sat for
judgment. He led worship. He blessed the people. He raised the son who
was not his.

For roughly a year, he did not say a word about it.

If you want to know what it cost him, you do not need to guess. He wrote
it down.

> *For when I kept silent, my bones wasted away through my groaning all day
> long.*
> *For day and night your hand was heavy upon me; my strength was dried up
> as by the heat of summer.* (Ps 32:3–4)

The Hebrew under *wasted away* is *balah* — the word you would use for a
coat that rots on the hook. Not the word for a coat that gets torn off
you. The word for a coat that decays slowly while you wear it.

The Hebrew under *heavy* is *kabed* — weight. The same root that gives the
Old Testament its word for glory, *kavod*. Glory is what is weighty. So
is unconfessed sin. It just does not bear up under its own weight the way
glory does.

David is telling us, from the inside of the year, that hidden sin is not
invisible sin. It does not stay in the room you put it in. It walks out
into your bones. It dries up the heat in your hands. It rots you slowly
while you keep wearing the same robe and sitting on the same throne and
singing the same psalms.

A year of that.

### 2. The breaking

Then the prophet Nathan walks into the throne room with a story about a
rich man and a poor man and a single ewe lamb. David does not see himself
in it until the last line. *You are the man.*

And David's answer is four words in the Hebrew. *Chatati la-YHWH.* I have
sinned against the LORD.

No excuse. No deflection. No "yes, but Bathsheba—." No "yes, but the
war—." No "yes, but I am the king and the king is permitted—." Just:
*chatati*. I sinned. Against the LORD.

Verse 5 of Psalm 32 says it the same way:

> *I acknowledged my sin to you, and I did not cover my iniquity; I said,
> "I will confess my transgressions to the LORD," and you forgave the
> iniquity of my sin.* (Ps 32:5)

Look at that verse carefully. He uses three different words for sin in one
sentence. He is not stuttering. He is naming every dimension of what he
did. That is where the psalm is going.

### 3. The blessing — three words for sin, three for grace

Go back to the first two verses. The Hebrew word that opens the psalm is
*ashrei*. Blessed. Happy. *O the blessedness of the man*—

> *Blessed is the one whose transgression is forgiven, whose sin is
> covered.*
> *Blessed is the man against whom the LORD counts no iniquity, and in
> whose spirit there is no deceit.* (Ps 32:1–2)

Three Hebrew words for sin. Three Hebrew words for what God does about it.
Six words. Pull them out.

#### Sin, named three ways

**Pesha.** Transgression. Willful rebellion. The boundary was there. You
saw it. You knew it was a boundary. You crossed it anyway. *Pesha* is not
weakness; it is defiance. It is the Hebrew word the prophets use of nations
that break covenant on purpose. David's adultery was a *pesha*. He knew
the law. He had written psalms about the law. He crossed it anyway.

**Chata'ah.** Sin in the sense of missing the mark. The verb *chata* is
used in Judges 20 of slingers in Gibeah who could sling a stone at a hair
and not miss. *Chata* is what they would do if they missed. It is falling
short of the target — falling short of the life you were made for. *All
have sinned and fall short of the glory of God* (Rom 3:23) is Paul giving
us *chata'ah* in Greek.

**Avon.** Iniquity. From the root *avah* — to bend, to twist. *Avon* is
not the act of sin so much as the bent condition of the heart that
produced the act. The moral distortion underneath the act. *The heart is
deceitful above all things, and desperately sick; who can understand it?*
(Jer 17:9) — that is *avon* talking.

David is not confessing one thing. He is naming every dimension of what
sin actually is. The act of rebellion. The miss of the mark. The twist of
the heart underneath the miss.

That is what the Bible means by sin. Not a mistake. Not a misjudgment.
Not a slip. All three of those, together.

#### Grace, named three ways

Now look at what God does in those same two verses.

**Nasa.** Lifted. Carried. *Whose transgression is forgiven* — the verb
is *nasui*, from *nasa*, to bear, to carry. The same verb Leviticus 16
uses for the scapegoat that *bears* the iniquity of the people away into
the wilderness. The same verb Isaiah 53 uses of the Servant: *Surely he
has borne our griefs and carried our sorrows.* Sin gets lifted. Not in
the abstract. Someone bends down and picks it up off you and walks away
with it.

**Kasah.** Covered. *Whose sin is covered.* Not hidden. Not swept under
the rug. The Hebrew priestly imagination behind this word is the blood of
the sacrifice covering the mercy seat — not making the sin invisible, but
placing a covering between the holy God and the unholy man. When Paul
reaches for a Greek word for what Christ is on the cross, he reaches for
*hilasterion* — the mercy seat. The place of covering. Romans 3:25.

**Lo yachshov.** Not counted. *Blessed is the man against whom the LORD
counts no iniquity.* The verb is *chashav* — to reckon, to credit, to
charge to an account. The same verb Genesis 15:6 uses of Abram: *he
believed the LORD, and he counted it to him as righteousness.* The same
verb, in its Greek translation, that Paul will use eleven times in Romans
4.

David is reaching for the ledger. He is saying: God keeps books. There
is a column for what I owe. And the entry is not there. The debt is not
concealed; the debt is not charged to the account at all.

Three words for sin. Three words for grace. Every dimension of what sin
is, met with a corresponding act of God.

### 4. Christ — where each of these becomes real

The three Hebrew words are not magic. They do not work because David is
clever, or because the Hebrew is beautiful, or because confession by
itself heals a soul. They work for one reason only. They describe what
God does *in Jesus Christ*.

Paul knew this. In Romans 4 he is arguing that Abraham was justified by
faith, not by works. He needs a second witness — a witness from the
Davidic line, from the heart of Israel's worship. So he reaches for the
very two verses we just read.

> *Blessed are those whose lawless deeds are forgiven, and whose sins are
> covered;*
> *blessed is the man against whom the Lord will not count his sin.*
> (Rom 4:7–8)

Paul quotes Psalm 32:1–2 word for word from the Greek Old Testament — and
then, eight verses later, he says:

> *That is why his faith was "counted to him as righteousness." But the
> words "it was counted to him" were not written for his sake alone, but
> for ours also. It will be counted to us who believe in him who raised
> from the dead Jesus our Lord, who was delivered up for our trespasses
> and raised for our justification.* (Rom 4:22–25)

That is where the psalm goes. David is reaching for an accounting verb in
Psalm 32 because Paul is going to reach for the same accounting verb in
Romans 4 to describe what happens at the cross.

**Nasa** — *lifted, carried* — becomes 1 Peter 2:24: *He himself bore our
sins in his body on the tree.* Christ is the one who walks away with the
load. Not metaphorically. Bodily. On the tree.

**Kasah** — *covered* — becomes Romans 3:25: *whom God put forward as a
propitiation by his blood, to be received by faith.* The blood of Jesus
is the covering between the holy God and the unholy man. Not concealment.
Atonement.

**Lo yachshov** — *not counted* — becomes Romans 4:5: *to the one who
does not work but believes in him who justifies the ungodly, his faith is
counted as righteousness.* The debt is not on your ledger because it is on
his. The righteousness on your ledger is not yours; it is his, reckoned to
you through faith.

The murderer and adulterer who wrote Psalm 32 was not making it up. He
was prophesying. He was telling Israel — and through Israel, telling us
— what God was going to do, one day, at a cross, in a garden tomb, on a
Sunday morning.

### 5. Call

Here is the question, and it is the only question that matters this
morning.

Whatever you are hiding — and you know what it is, I do not need to guess
— *what is the column it is sitting in?*

There are two columns. One says: *I will carry this. I will keep silent.
My bones will rot in this robe and my strength will dry up like summer
heat, but I will not say a word, because if I say it out loud I will lose
what I have built.* That is David, year one.

The other column says: *I acknowledged my sin to you, and I did not cover
my iniquity.* (Ps 32:5) That is David, year two. And on the other side
of those words, the LORD forgave the iniquity of his sin.

The gospel is not that you confess and God meets you halfway. The gospel
is that Christ bore the sin — *nasa*. Christ's blood covers the sinner —
*kasah*. The Father does not reckon the sin to the one who is in Christ
— *lo yachshov*. The work is already done. The only thing left for you
to do is what David did when Nathan walked in. Stop carrying it. Bring
it. Confess it. Trust the one who carried it for you.

If you have never come to Christ — come. The blessedness of Psalm 32 is
not for the deserving. It is for the one against whom the LORD *would
have* counted iniquity, and now does not, because the iniquity was counted
to Christ on the tree and the righteousness of Christ was counted to faith.

If you are a Christian still carrying something — drop it. You are not
protecting anything by keeping silent. You are rotting in the robe. The
hand you feel pressing down on you is not the hand of an angry judge. It
is the hand of a Father who refuses to let you live as if the sin were
yours to carry when his Son already carried it.

David waited a year. You do not have to.

> *Therefore let everyone who is godly offer prayer to you at a time when
> you may be found; surely in the rush of great waters, they shall not
> reach him.*
> *You are a hiding place for me; you preserve me from trouble; you
> surround me with shouts of deliverance.* (Ps 32:6–7)

Let us pray.
