## Essay 1: Believer's Baptism (2,500–3,000 words)

### Your Conviction (From Sermons)
- Baptism is for believers only, not infants
- Luke 23: "If baptism were necessary for salvation, the thief would have been lost"
- Baptism is symbolic, not salvific
- Personal faith profession required

### Essay Structure

**Introduction (300 words)**
- Hook: Why does this matter? (credobaptism splits churches from Reformation onward)
- Thesis: Believer's baptism is the biblical pattern; requires personal faith
- Preview: Historical, scriptural, systematic, pastoral sections

**Historical Context (400 words)**
- Why Baptists separated from Reformed on this point
- Calvin & Westminster believed in household/infant baptism
- English Baptists (1600s) insisted on believer's baptism
- This is your heritage; understand why

**Scriptural Foundation (800 words)**
- Romans 6:1-4 — Baptized into his death (personal union with Christ)
- Colossians 2:11-12 — Circumcision of Christ; believer's experience
- Acts 2:38-39 — Repent, believe, baptized (sequence matters)
- Matthew 28:19-20 — Teach, then baptize (faith precedes baptism)
- 1 Peter 3:18-22 — Not water that saves; resurrection saves
- Mark 16:16 — Believes and baptized (belief first)

**Systematic Theology (600 words)**
- Relationship between baptism and salvation (symbolism, not causation)
- Baptism as sign & seal of covenant with Christ
- Role of faith in making baptism meaningful
- Why infant baptism cannot work (no personal faith)
- Baptism & church membership (confession required)

**Pastoral Application (500 words)**
- Why this matters for church discipline & membership
- The joy of baptism as personal testimony
- How to counsel those from paedobaptist backgrounds
- Baptism as reaffirmation of faith; comfort to the baptized
- Preventing both legalism (must be baptized to be saved) and indifference (baptism doesn't matter)

**Conclusion (200 words)**
- Restate: Believer's baptism is biblically clear; reformation necessity
- Pastoral: Not divisive boundary, but beautiful testimony
- Forward: Preaching will deepen understanding

### Tone
- Pastoral, not polemical (not attacking paedobaptists)
- Scholarly foundation (understand history & Scripture), but accessible language
- Warm conviction, not cold doctrine

### Key Challenge
Avoid the "infant baptism is obviously wrong" tone. Instead: "Here's why we believe Scripture points toward believer's baptism; here's the pastoral beauty of that conviction."

## Essay 2: Congregational Polity (2,500–3,000 words)

### Your Conviction (From Sermons)
- Church authority is congregational, not hierarchical
- Elders lead; congregation affirms
- Prayer for leaders; respect for authority; but congregation has final voice
- Implied in 1 Tim 2 (Nero is king, but God is ultimate)

### Essay Structure

**Introduction (300 words)**
- Hook: What does church government have to do with your soul's health?
- Thesis: Congregational polity protects the flock; prevents tyranny
- Preview: Historical, scriptural, systematic, pastoral sections

**Historical Context (400 words)**
- Baptist heritage: Congregational from 1600s (English Baptists)
- Why split from Presbyterian/Episcopal models?
- How congregational polity shaped Baptist identity
- Modern challenges (megachurch, celebrity pastors)

**Scriptural Foundation (800 words)**
- Matthew 18:15-20 — Church binds/looses; corporate authority
- Acts 6:1-6 — Congregation chooses deacons (voice in leadership)
- Acts 15:1-31 — Jerusalem Council; congregation makes decision
- 1 Timothy 3:1-7 — Elder qualifications (what to look for)
- Titus 1:5-9 — Elders appointed (plural; checks & balances)
- 1 Peter 5:1-4 — Elders as shepherds, not lords
- Hebrews 13:17 — Submit, but leaders answer to God

**Systematic Theology (600 words)**
- How congregational polity differs from Presbyterian (elders govern completely) and Episcopal (bishop governs)
- Elder plurality (checks against one-man rule)
- Congregation as ultimate voice (binding/loosing through agreement)
- Deacons as servants, not lords (1 Tim 3:8-12)
- Church discipline as congregational act (2 Cor 2:6, Matthew 18)
- Why this prevents both tyranny and chaos

**Pastoral Application (500 words)**
- How congregational polity protects church members
- Leadership accountability (leaders answer to congregation + God)
- Member voice matters (not passive reception)
- Preventing abuse: shepherding vs. controlling
- Building culture of mutual accountability
- Why this is good news for the flock

**Conclusion (200 words)**
- Restate: Congregational polity is biblical; protects souls
- Pastoral: Not perfect system, but reflects gospel
- Forward: Preaching will show how this works in practice

### Tone
- Protective, not combative (not attacking presbyterian/episcopal)
- Practical (show why this matters for real people)
- Defensive of congregational distinctiveness

### Key Challenge
Avoid both extremes: "Congregationalism is perfect" and "We're chaotic without leaders." Show healthy tension between elder leadership and congregational voice.

## Essay 3: Predestination & Election (3,000–3,500 words)

### Your Conviction (From Sermons)
- God is sovereign; chooses whom he will
- But human choice is real ("you chose to believe")
- Antinomy, not paradox (both true, even if we can't reconcile completely)
- Pastoral: God is in control; trust him

### Essay Structure

**Introduction (400 words)**
- Hook: Is God sovereign or are we free? (The tension)
- Thesis: Both are true; Scripture teaches both; we can hold both pastorally
- Preview: Historical, scriptural, systematic, pastoral sections

**Historical Context (500 words)**
- Augustine vs. Pelagius (grace vs. works)
- Aquinas & Medieval synthesis
- Reformation: Luther & Calvin on sovereignty
- Arminius & Arminianism (reaction)
- Reformed Baptist position (5-point Calvinism, modified)
- Why Ken's position

**Scriptural Foundation (1,000 words)**
- Ephesians 1:3-14 — Chosen before foundation; predestined to adoption
- Romans 8:28-30 — Foreknew, predestined, called, justified, glorified
- Romans 9:14-23 — God's sovereignty; Potter & clay (but not unjust)
- 2 Timothy 1:9 — Grace given before time began
- 1 Peter 1:1-2 — Chosen according to foreknowledge of God
- Matthew 23:37 — Jesus weeps over Jerusalem; they *refused* (real choice)
- John 6:37-44 — "All who come to me..."; "No one can come unless..."
- Acts 13:48 — Believed; *appointed* for eternal life (timing unclear)

**Systematic Theology (800 words)**
- Definitions: Predestination vs. Foreknowledge vs. Election
- God's sovereignty (foreknew all things; nothing surprises him)
- Human responsibility (real choice; real consequences)
- Antinomy explained (two truths that seem contradictory but both true)
- Why not fatalism (God's foreknowledge ≠ coercion)
- Why not Semi-Pelagianism (God's grace is necessary; man cannot choose God apart from grace)
- 5-point Calvinism framework: Total depravity, unconditional election, limited atonement, irresistible grace, perseverance
- Where Ken might nuance (willing to dialogue on some points)

**Pastoral Application (600 words)**
- How predestination gives assurance (God chose you; can't lose it)
- How predestination motivates evangelism (we don't know who's elect; proclaim gospel)
- How predestination comforts in suffering (God's plan is good)
- How predestination prevents pride (you didn't choose yourself)
- How to preach both election and free offer (yes, to all; but God saves by grace)
- Honest tension: We can't figure out the mechanics, but we trust God

**Conclusion (300 words)**
- Restate: God is sovereign; we have real choice; hold both pastorally
- Avoid: Over-systematizing mystery
- Forward: Preaching will show how this works in practice; comfort & motivation

### Tone
- Intellectually serious (this is complex; don't pretend it's simple)
- Honest about tension (we don't have all answers)
- Pastoral emphasis (why this matters for souls; comfort, not confusion)

### Key Challenge
This is the hardest essay. Don't try to *solve* predestination; instead, show how Ken holds the tensions biblically and pastorally. Resist over-systematizing. Let Scripture tension remain.

## Essay 4: Law & Gospel (2,500–3,000 words)

### Your Conviction (From Sermons)
- Law reveals sin; Gospel reveals grace
- Both are God's Word; both are merciful
- Gospel doesn't abolish Law; fulfills it (Matthew 5:17)
- Prevents legalism and antinomianism

### Essay Structure

**Introduction (300 words)**
- Hook: "Legalism or Libertinism?" (False dichotomy Ken wants to escape)
- Thesis: Law & Gospel are distinct but complementary; both from God
- Preview: Historical, scriptural, systematic, pastoral sections

**Historical Context (400 words)**
- Luther's insight: Law/Gospel distinction as core reform
- Reformed tradition: How they handle Law & Gospel
- Baptist tradition: Grace emphasis (sometimes losing Law)
- Modern confusion: Antinomianism on one side; legalism on other

**Scriptural Foundation (800 words)**
- Romans 3:19-26 — Law shows sin; Gospel shows grace (clear distinction)
- Galatians 3:19-29 — Law as tutor/pedagogue to Christ (purpose of Law)
- Matthew 5:17 — Not abolish Law; fulfill (Gospel completes, not cancels)
- Hebrews 8:6-13 — Old covenant, new covenant (both God's Word)
- James 1:25 — Law of liberty (obedience is freedom, not slavery)
- 2 Corinthians 3:4-11 — Letter kills; Spirit gives life (Law without grace kills)
- Romans 6:14 — Not under Law, under grace (freedom, not license)
- Titus 2:11-14 — Grace teaches us to say "No" to sin (grace produces obedience)

**Systematic Theology (600 words)**
- Law: Reveals God's standard; exposes sin; shows what we owe
- Gospel: Announces grace; Christ's work; enables what Law demands
- Relationship: Gospel answers Law's demands; fulfills Law's purpose
- Both are merciful: Law protects (civil function); Gospel saves
- How they prevent errors:
  - Law prevents antinomianism ("I'm free; I can sin")
  - Gospel prevents legalism ("I must earn God's favor")
- Preaching both: "This is what God requires; here's how Christ met it; here's how grace enables us to respond"

**Pastoral Application (500 words)**
- For the legalist: Gospel breaks bondage; you cannot earn favor
- For the libertine: Gospel frees to obey; sin hurts you
- How to counsel differently depending on the person's drift
- Building church culture that honors both Law & Gospel
- Preventing false guilt and false freedom

**Conclusion (200 words)**
- Restate: Law & Gospel are both God's Word; both essential
- Pastoral: Not a puzzle to solve; a rhythm to preach
- Forward: Sermons will show how to preach both in same passage

### Tone
- Balanced, not polemical
- Clear distinctions, but showing unity
- Practical (how to actually preach this)

### Key Challenge
Avoid letting Gospel erase Law (antinomianism) or Law dominate Gospel (legalism). Show how they work together.

## Essay 5: Common Grace (2,000–2,500 words)

### Your Conviction (From Sermons)
- God shows kindness to all (saved and unsaved)
- Illustrates God's character
- Distinct from saving grace
- Shapes how we engage the world

### Essay Structure

**Introduction (250 words)**
- Hook: God sends rain on the just and unjust (Matthew 5:45)
- Thesis: Common grace is real; distinct from saving grace; shapes Christian living
- Preview: Historical, scriptural, systematic, pastoral sections

**Historical Context (300 words)**
- Calvin's concept of common grace
- How it shaped Reformed public theology
- Baptist application: How to think about non-believers' good works
- Modern relevance: Cultural engagement, common good, pluralism

**Scriptural Foundation (600 words)**
- Matthew 5:45 — Rain on just and unjust (clearest passage)
- Acts 14:16-17 — God gives rain, seasons, food; testimony to all
- Romans 2:4 — God's kindness leads to repentance (common grace is real)
- Titus 2:11 — Grace of God appeared to all (grace visible to all)
- Romans 1:19-20 — God's invisible attributes seen in creation
- James 1:17 — Every good gift from above (God provides to all)
- 1 Peter 2:13-14 — Government; God's provision for order (common grace)

**Systematic Theology (500 words)**
- Definition: Common grace is God's kindness to all creatures (saved & unsaved)
- Distinct from saving grace: Doesn't save; doesn't change heart; doesn't justify
- But real: Preserves creation, enables civil law, allows good works, testifies to God
- Function: Prevents total chaos; allows humans to do good without faith
- Illustrates God's character: Patience, kindness, mercy to all
- How it relates to special grace: Precedes it; prepares for it; makes final judgment just

**Pastoral Application (400 words)**
- Recognizing God's hand in non-Christian life (good art, science, ethics)
- Engaging culture without compromise (common grace allows partnership)
- Humility in witnessing (non-believers can do good; that doesn't save them)
- Gratitude for common grace (rain, food, safety, beauty, order)
- Preventing both triumphalism ("only Christians are good") and syncretism ("all good is equally grace")

**Conclusion (150 words)**
- Restate: Common grace is real, distinct, important
- Pastoral: Changes how we see the world; builds humility & gratitude
- Forward: Sermons will show common grace in daily life

### Tone
- Warm, grateful (appreciating God's kindness)
- Humble (non-Christians can do real good)
- But clear boundaries (common grace ≠ saving grace)

### Key Challenge
Avoid both extremes: "Non-believers are totally depraved so they do no real good" and "All good works are equally valid." Show common grace as real but limited.

## Tone Guidelines (All Essays)

✓ **Do:**
- Write in Ken's pastoral voice (as if from the pulpit, but in essay form)
- Ground everything in Scripture
- Show why each doctrine matters (not just abstract theology)
- Acknowledge tension/complexity where it exists
- Be warm and inviting, not cold or academic
- Use concrete examples where possible

✗ **Don't:**
- Sound like a seminary textbook
- Attack other positions (be charitable to Reformers, Calvinists, etc.)
- Pretend questions are simpler than they are
- Ignore pastoral implications
- Write impersonally ("one might argue..."; use "I" sometimes)
- Over-argue minor points
