## TL;DR

All 7 photographed devices are in the **McGill paper-fastener / staple-press
family** (George W. McGill, New York; manufactured by Holmes, Booth &
Haydens of Waterbury, CT and New York). They span three McGill patent
families:

- **US 67,665** (1867) — McGill's Patent Fastener Press (tabletop,
  spring-plunger + hammer-punch, for driving brass split-pin fasteners)
- **US 212,316** (Feb 18 1879) — Single-Stroke Staple Press No. 1 —
  **four patented mechanical embodiments** under this one patent
- **US 252,841** (Jan 24 1882) — Staple Press No. 2 — octagonal removable
  plunger-case, enables perpendicular staple orientation

All three patents were cast, japanned, and decorated by Holmes, Booth &
Haydens during the 1879–1913 advertising window. The model line was
sold as **No. 1, No. 1a, and No. 2** (confirmed by period staple-binder
packaging labeled for all three).

## 1. What is actually in US 212,316 (1879)

Direct reading of the patent drawings (not derivative sources). The patent
describes **four distinct mechanical embodiments**, each protected under
the same patent:

1. **Standard two-prong configuration** (Figs. 1–18) — bed-plate +
   rear-pillar + vibrating lever; plunger with feathered foot; rectangular
   anvil with transverse groove for inward-clinching staple legs.
2. **Dual-groove variant** (Figs. 19–22) — modified case allowing
   perpendicular staple orientation for pamphlet binding.
3. **Reversed-groove design** (Figs. 27–29) — spreads staple legs outward
   (not inward) — for a different clinch pattern.
4. **Adjustable anvil/pillar** (Figs. 30–34) — screw-threaded adjustment
   for varying paper-stack thickness.

The patent explicitly protects the *mechanism*, not the *cast body*.
Ornamental body designs varied at the foundry and were hand-decorated at
the Holmes-Booth-Haydens Waterbury japanning shop. Multiple body castings
were used concurrently — which is why the same patent date appears on
devices with visibly different frame geometry (horseshoe / D-bracket /
inverted-U / S-scroll with ornamental finials).

The practical implication for the 7 devices: different frame profiles do
not mean different makers. They may all be McGill No. 1 devices under US
212,316, each a different foundry pattern / production year / decorative
variant.

## 2. What is actually in US 252,841 (1882) — the No. 2

Four figures, single embodiment. The distinguishing feature is an
**externally-octagonal removable plunger case** that slots into an
octagonal socket in the base, allowing the staple to be oriented either
crosswise or lengthwise along the base plate, and also allowing the case
to be removed and used as a standalone driver (without clinching) for
upholstery, carpet-laying, etc.

Diagnostic test for No. 2 vs. No. 1: if the plunger housing is octagonal
and removable from the base, it is a No. 2 (US 252,841). If the plunger is
fixed within the frame, it is a No. 1 (US 212,316).

## 3. What McGill actually exhibited at the 1876 Centennial

This is a **Round-4-new finding** worth documenting: the Single-Stroke
Staple Press was not patented until **February 1879**, three years *after*
the Centennial. So the first-prize medal McGill won at the 1876
Philadelphia Centennial Exhibition **cannot have been for the Staple
Press**.

What he actually showed in 1876:
- **The brass paper fastener itself** (US 56,587, 1866) — the bent-wire
  split-pin
- **The Fastener Press** (US 67,665, 1867) — tabletop spring-plunger press
  for driving those fasteners
- **The Paper Fastener Punch** (US 151,236, 1874) — small handheld brass
  desk item

1876 = Fastener Press era. 1879+ = Staple Press era. This matters for
dating any of the 7 devices: if the base lettering reads "Pat'd 1867" (or
similar) the device is pre-1879 Fastener Press, not a Staple Press.

## 4. Lawrence v. McGill (1893) — English prior-art claim

Another Round-4-new thread: in the 1893 infringement suit *Lawrence v.
McGill*, Benjamin Lawrence of the B. Lawrence Stationery Company defended
against infringement of McGill's US 56,587 (1866) and US 285,640 (1883)
patents by arguing that the brass paper-fastener design was **"invented in
England"** — i.e., prior art. McGill sent circular notices to the trade;
Lawrence sold McGill-pattern fasteners at half price.

Implication: there is English prior art for the McGill split-pin paper
fastener. That prior art would be the subject of a targeted search in the
**British Patent Office pre-1866 specifications** — probably via the
*Abridgments of Specifications* series for "stationery" or "fastening"
classes.

## 5. The UK Eagle Staple Press — unidentified manufacturer

The Eagle Staple Press No. 1 was McGill's simultaneous UK release of the
1879 Single-Stroke Staple Press. Same mechanism, different painted
decoration. **Its UK manufacturer is not identified** in any of the
public sources I have consulted:

- Derby Museums catalog record: does not identify UK manufacturer
- Whangārei Museum (NZ): does not identify UK manufacturer
- Market Lavington Museum (UK): does not identify UK manufacturer
- McGill/Holmes-Booth-Haydens US sources: reference the UK version but
  do not identify its foundry

This is a genuine research gap. Plausible candidate firms (given the
1879–1913 period, cast-iron japanned small-stationery-press expertise,
and US-licensed product lines): **Birmingham** (Holmans/BSA group
foundries; possibly M. Myers & Son, later W.H. Tonks — but unverified
for this product) or **Sheffield** (unlikely — they focused on cutlery).
Resolution would require inspection of a UK-marked Eagle body for
stamped / cast-in UK foundry marks, and then a Birmingham/London
trade-directory cross-check.

## 7. What would turn LOW/MEDIUM into HIGH

Scaglione likely already knows this list, but recording it for the
written record:

1. **Top-down photograph of each base** — the gilt lettering
   ("McGill's Patent Single Stroke Staple Press Patented Feb. 18, 1879"
   or similar) is diagnostic for No. 1 vs. No. 1a vs. No. 2.
2. **Plunger-case geometry** — is the external profile octagonal
   (No. 2) or cylindrical within a fixed frame (No. 1)?
3. **Magnet test on P4** — brass is non-magnetic; cast iron is
   strongly magnetic. Rules out the brass-premium-edition hypothesis.
4. **Weight** — No. 1 ≈ 2 lb 10 oz; No. 2 slightly lighter due to
   the smaller removable case; Fastener Press (1867) is a different
   weight class entirely.
5. **P5 — count anvil dimensions** — if the anvil is round with a
   single central punch socket (not a staple-clinching groove), it is
   a **Fastener Press (US 67,665, 1867)**, not a Staple Press.

## 10. Patent-expiration anchors and the 1891 invalidation

Under the 1861 Patent Act, US utility patents ran 17 years from issue.
So:

- **US 212,316** (Feb 18 1879) expires **Feb 18 1896**
- **US 252,841** (Jan 24 1882) expires **Jan 24 1899**

Meaning: **after Feb 1896 any foundry could legally cast the No. 1 form**;
after Jan 1899, the No. 2.

The earlier invalidation was *McGill Fastener Company v. Universal Paper
Fastener Co.* (1891), which voided **US 162,183** (1875) and **US 337,182**
(1886) for "want of novelty" (per Smithsonian Lemelson Center, Alison
Oswald, 2014). That invalidation hit McGill's *fastener-piece* patents,
not the press patents — but signaled that the "McGill-pattern" designs
were becoming fair game for copyists.

The compound effect: by the end of 1899, *every* McGill press form was
out of patent protection. Competitors could cast copies. Non-McGill
foundries could put their own decoration on the same frame and sell it
legally. **Post-1899, unmarked tabletop McGill-pattern staplers can't
be assumed to be McGill production.**

### Implications for P1–P7 dating

1. **If a device bears clear McGill base-lettering** ("MCGILL'S PATENT
   SINGLE STROKE STAPLE PRESS PATD FEB 18 1879") — high confidence
   authentic McGill production.
2. **If decoration suggests post-1896 production and no base-lettering
   is visible** — could be authentic McGill Fastener Co. (1899+), a
   competitor copy, or an unmarked HBH run-off.
3. **P5's S-scroll+ball-finial frame is a plausible post-1896 copyist
   variant** — worth specifically testing: if the base has no McGill
   lettering, it may be a post-expiration unmarked foundry casting,
   not a McGill.

### UK Chancery 1881 — new research thread

England is the clearest documented UK-legal-proceeding record for McGill.
Searching the **UK National Archives Chancery records (C-series)** for

since McGill's case would have had to name his own UK-licensed producer
